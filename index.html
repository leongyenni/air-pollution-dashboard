Silent Killer in the Air: Air Pollution

by Leong Yen Ni

Outdoor air pollution is one of the world’s largest health and environmental problems – one that tends to worsen for countries as they industralize and transition from low to middle incomes. The Global Burden of Disease study estimates that 4506193 deaths were attributed to outdoor air pollution in the latest year.1 There is also growing evidence that long-term exposure to air pollution can have important impacts on other aspects of health and wellbeing – such as cognitive function.

Risk Factors and Their Impact on Mortality
Air pollution is ranked as Top 3 death cause globally, with a rate of 86.23 deaths per 100,000 people. This statistic indicates that air pollution is a significant global health issue, contributing to a substantial number of deaths worldwide.
Ranking of Air Pollution as a Cause of Death in Different Countries
Air pollution has consistently ranked as one of the top two leading causes of death in both India and China over the years. Notably, air pollution has also had a significant impact on mortality in Malaysia, where it is ranked as the fourth leading cause of death in 2019.

Death Rates from Different Types of Air Pollutions
Ambient particulate matter (PM) pollution is the most harmful form of air pollution, with a death rate of 309 per 100,000 people, in comparison to other pollutants such as ambient ozone pollution (35) and household air pollution (111).
According to the United States Environmental Protection Agency (2022), PM pollution consists of tiny particles that can easily penetrate our respiratory system and enter the bloodstream, leading to severe health issues, particularly affecting the lungs and heart. In contrast, ozone and indoor household pollutants are typically composed of gases or larger particles that do not penetrate as deeply as PM pollutants.
Type of Air Pollution Description
Ambient Ozone Pollution Ozone is a harmful pollutant formed through chemical reactions between pollutants emited from vehicle, industrial emissions and fossil fuels.
Ambient Particulate Matter Pollution Particular matter (PM) is a mixture of solid or liquid particles in the air. These particules are small enough to pass through our lungs and into the bloodstream through inhalation.
  • PM2.5: Particles with a diameter of 2.5 micrometres or less. PM10 includes PM2.5.
  • PM10: Particles with a diameter of 10 micrometres or less.
Indoor Pollution Indoor air contamination in homes or workplaces from sources like cooking fumes, tobacco smoke or household products.
How Are Death Rates From Air Pollution Changing?
Encouragingly, the death rate from air pollution has been steadily declining over the years, implying positive progress in improving air quality to safeguard public health.
