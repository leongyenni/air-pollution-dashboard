Silent Killer in the Air: Air Pollution

by Leong Yen Ni

Air pollution stands as a substantial global environmental challenge, posing a significant threat to public health. It is reported that air pollutation contributes to more than 6.5 million deaths worldwide each year.

In this visualisation dashboard, we will investigate the impact of air pollution on human lives across the world, explore the types and sources of air pollutants and analyze the trends in death rates attributed to air pollution.

Various Types of Air Pollution

Which type of air pollution has the most devastating impact and leads to the highest rate of deaths?

Chart 4: Death Rate from Different Types of Air Pollutions

Ambient particulate matter (PM) pollution is the most harmful form of air pollution, with a death rate of 309 per 100,000 people, in comparison to other pollutants such as ambient ozone pollution (35) and household air pollution (111).


According to the United States Environmental Protection Agency (2022), PM pollution consists of tiny particles that can easily penetrate our respiratory system and enter the bloodstream, leading to severe health issues, particularly affecting the lungs and heart. In contrast, ozone and indoor household pollutants are typically composed of gases or larger particles that do not penetrate as deeply as PM pollutants.

Type of Air Pollution Description
Ambient Ozone Pollution Ozone is a harmful pollutant formed through chemical reactions between pollutants emited from vehicle, industrial emissions and fossil fuels.
Ambient Particulate Matter Pollution Particular matter (PM) is a mixture of solid or liquid particles in the air. These particules are small enough to pass through our lungs and into the bloodstream through inhalation.
  • PM2.5: Particles with a diameter of 2.5 micrometres or less. PM10 includes PM2.5.
  • PM10: Particles with a diameter of 10 micrometres or less.
Indoor Pollution Indoor air contamination in homes or workplaces from sources like cooking fumes, tobacco smoke or household products.

Linking between the Death Rate from Ambient Particulate Matter (PM) Pollution & Exposure to PM 2.5 Air Pollution

What is Their Relationship?

Chart 5: Death Rate from Ambient Particulate Matter Pollution vs PM 2.5 Air Pollution Annual Mean Exposure (µg/m³)

According to the visualisation above, we can observe that the death rate from PM (Particulate Matter) pollution increases with greater exposure to PM 2.5 air pollution, indicating a positive correlation. Furthermore, countries in Asia and Africa have higher levels of exposure to PM 2.5 pollution. Therefore, we can conclude that the high death rates from air pollution in China, Malaysia, and India are likely due to the high emissions of PM 2.5 pollutants in these countries.
